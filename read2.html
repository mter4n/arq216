Why Loads Exist

Salvadori imagines a world where structure wouldn't be necessary ‚Äî and explains why our world is different.

If the earth did not pull, the wind did not blow, the earth's surface did not shake or sink, and the air temperature did not change, loads would not exist and structure would be unnecessary.

‚Äî Salvadori, "Why Buildings Stand Up" (1980), p. 43

The Alice-in-Wonderland World

In a fantasy world without gravity, wind, or earthquakes, architects could focus only on defining space. But in our world, every building must fight these forces constantly.

This is the fundamental reality: the earth pulls everything down. Structure exists to redirect that pull safely to the ground.

Structure supports all the loads that act, unavoidably, on buildings. The engineer's first job is to determine which loads will act on a structure and how strong they might be in extreme cases.

‚Äî Salvadori, p. 43

Action & Reaction

Newton's Third Law is the foundation of load path thinking: every force creates an equal and opposite reaction.

Newton's 3rd Law

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction

‚Üì Weight W (Action)
üè¢
‚Üë Reaction R (Ground pushes back)

If a building with a weight W is placed on the ground, we can say that the building is exerting a downward force of W on the ground. However, for the building to remain stable on the resisting ground surface without sinking completely, the ground must resist with an upward force of equal magnitude.

‚Äî Onouye & Kane, "Statics and Strength of Materials" (2012), p. 16

Think about what this means: if the ground pushed back with less force than the building's weight, the building would sink. If it pushed with more, the building would levitate. The ground must react with exactly equal force.

When you place weight on your tower, the table pushes back with equal force. Your tower is the path between these two forces.

A structure consists of heavy elements like columns, beams, floors, arches, or domes which must, first of all, support their own weight, the so-called dead load.

‚Äî Salvadori, p. 43

ü§î The Paradox of Structural Design

To know how strong a beam must be, you need to know its weight. But to know its weight, you need to know its size. And to know its size, you need to know how strong it must be. This is why structural design is learned through experience.

In addition to its dead load, a structure must support a variety of other weights ‚Äî people, furniture, equipment, stored goods. These impermanent or live loads may be shifted around and they may change in value.

‚Äî Salvadori, p. 44

Salvadori notes that we can never know exactly what the live load is or how it will be distributed. Building codes establish conservative values based on the worst conditions expected during a building's entire life.

Every connection, every joint, every member in the path must be strong enough to carry the load. If one element fails ‚Äî even if everything else is overdesigned ‚Äî the entire path breaks.

Whenever a body A exerts a force on another body B, body B will resist with an equal magnitude but in the opposite direction.

‚Äî Onouye & Kane, p. 16

This applies at every connection in your tower. Where one stick meets another, the forces must transfer completely. A weak joint doesn't just reduce strength ‚Äî it creates a gap in the load path.
