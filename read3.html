Where Structures Fail

Engineers design members carefully ‚Äî beams, columns, slabs. But the most dangerous points are where these members meet.

Many of the structural failures in buildings and bridges occur in the connection of members and not in the members themselves.

‚Äî Onouye & Kane, "Statics and Strength of Materials" (2012), p. 508

Think about it: a beam might be perfectly sized, a column perfectly strong. But if the bolt connecting them fails, it doesn't matter. The load path breaks at the joint.

Structural members must be joined in such a way that permits the safe transfer of loads from one member to another.

‚Äî Onouye & Kane, p. 508

Connections do more than hold pieces together ‚Äî they transfer forces. Every newton of load must pass through the joint from one member to the next. If the connection can't handle that transfer, the structure fails.

At 7:05 P.M. on Friday, July 17, 1981, the atrium was filled with more than sixteen hundred people, most of them dancing to the music of a well-known band for a tea dance competition, when suddenly a frightening, sharp sound like a thunderbolt was heard, stopping the dancers in mid-step.
