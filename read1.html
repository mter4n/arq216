Defining Structure

Definition

Structure is something made up of interdependent parts in a definite pattern of organization ‚Äî achieving the most strength from the least material.

This definition comes from Onouye & Kane, but notice the key insight: structure isn't just about holding things up. It's about efficiency ‚Äî maximum strength, minimum material.

The primary function of a building structure is to support and redirect loads and forces safely to the ground. Building structures are constantly withstanding the forces of wind, the effects of gravity, vibrations, and sometimes even earthquakes.

‚Äî Onouye & Kane, "Statics and Strength of Materials" (2012), p. 1

Salvadori takes a different approach ‚Äî he separates function (what a building does) from structure (what holds it up):

The structural components of a building assure that the elements required to fulfill its function will stand up. Columns, beams, and floors ‚Äî structure ‚Äî make possible the architectural function.

‚Äî Salvadori, "Why Buildings Stand Up" (1980), p. 19

Nature's Lessons

The best structures aren't invented ‚Äî they're discovered by observing nature.

Nature does nothing in vain, and more is in vain when less will serve; for Nature is pleased with simplicity, and affects not the pomp of superfluous causes.

‚Äî Sir Isaac Newton, Principia (quoted in Onouye & Kane, p. 1)

Look around: every natural form is a structural lesson. A spider web, a seashell, the human skeleton ‚Äî each achieves incredible strength with minimal material.

03

Intuition Over Equations

Good structural design comes from intuition first, mathematics second.

Good structural design is more related to correct intuitive sense than to sets of complex mathematical equations. Mathematics should be merely a convenient and validating tool by which the designer determines the physical sizes and proportions.

‚Äî Onouye & Kane, p. 2

Both sources agree: you already understand structure intuitively. Salvadori explains that ancient builders had no theory ‚Äî just physical intuition developed through experience.

Structures, even large and daring structures, were built in the past by craftsmen who had no theoretical knowledge and moved on a purely physical intuition of structural principles. We all possess these intuitions through our daily experience.

‚Äî Salvadori, p. 25

How did they learn the limits? Through failure.

Both nature and human builders learn the same way: try, fail, adapt. Your Robot analysis replaces centuries of trial ‚Äî but the principle remains.

Structure's Beauty

When structure is honest and efficient, beauty emerges naturally.

The objective data of the problem, technology and statics, suggest the solutions and forms; the aesthetic sensitivity of the designer, who understands the intrinsic beauty and validity, welcomes the suggestion and models it, emphasizes it, proportions it.

‚Äî Pier Luigi Nervi, "Aesthetics and Technology in Architecture" (quoted in Onouye & Kane, p. 3)

Salvadori tells of Nervi seeing the George Washington Bridge for the first time:

To think that before it was built man had never seen such lovely curves!

‚Äî Pier Luigi Nervi (quoted in Salvadori, p. 26)

The curves weren't designed to be beautiful ‚Äî they emerged from the forces. A correct structure isn't automatically beautiful, but:

Structure has a message of its own: it can be a message of strength or elegance, of waste or economy, of ugliness or beauty. But without it, architecture cannot exist.

‚Äî Salvadori, p. 26

üí° The Principle

Structural beauty cannot exist without structural correctness. Make your tower work first ‚Äî and it may become beautiful.
